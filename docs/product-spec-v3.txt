Cartpool

Product Concept & Feature Spec

Version 3 — July 19, 2026

Changes from v2

Version 3 incorporates four corrections identified in review of the v2
spec and its technical addendum. Everything else carries over from v2
unchanged.

-   Self-purchase allowed. The addendum's item state machine restricted
    marking an item purchased to “any member other than the adder.” That
    restriction is dropped: any active member, including the adder, can
    mark an open item purchased. A solo list only works if you can check
    off your own items, and on shared lists the shopper routinely buys
    their own items on the same trip. (See Section 4.)

-   Solo list is a real one-member group. v2 counted the solo list as
    one of the 3 free groups, but the data model represented solo items
    with a null group — leaving no group row to count. The solo list is
    now an actual group with one member, created at signup. Group
    counting works naturally, every item has a group, and
    merge-on-first-invite reduces to a simple item move. (See Sections 2
    and 9.)

-   Blocking co-placement is now bidirectional. A deliberate behavior
    change from v2's one-directional rule: after A blocks B, neither can
    join a group containing the other. One-directional blocking would
    have let B follow A into A's other groups, defeating the purpose.
    Removal and notification behavior is unchanged and still
    one-directional — only A leaves shared groups, and B is never
    notified. (See Section 3.)

-   Downgrade freeze scope clarified. When a paid subscription lapses
    with more than 3 groups, the user is read-only everywhere until they
    confirm which 3 groups to keep; after confirming, the kept 3 return
    to full read-write and only the excess groups remain read-only. (See
    Section 9.)

-   Implementation note added. The 4-active-member cap and
    first-come-first-served waitlist promotion are race-prone under
    concurrent joins and cannot be plain declarative database
    constraints — they require a trigger or serialized insert check.
    (See Section 3.)

1. Overview

Cartpool is a shopping list app for iOS and Android. On the surface it
looks and works like a simple, personal shopping list. The twist: up to
four people (family, friends, neighbors) can form a group and share one
running list. Anyone in the group can add items for themselves, and
anyone going shopping can pick up and check off items for everyone else.
Payment for picked-up items is settled outside the app (cash, Venmo, in
person) — Cartpool only tracks who wants what and who bought it.

A secondary but central use case: bulk-store shopping (Costco, Sam's
Club, etc.). One person buys a bulk item and tags it as shared; other
group members opt in with a single tap, no quantity math required.

Primary audience

-   Anyone who wants a normal personal shopping list, with the option to
    loop in others.

-   Small friend/family/neighbor pods who informally shop for each
    other.

-   Elderly users living alone or with limited mobility, who rely on a
    neighbor or family member to pick things up — accessibility is a
    first-class design constraint, not an add-on.

2. Core User Journey

-   Download the app and sign in with a phone number. A personal solo
    group — a real group with you as its only member — is created
    automatically. It looks and works like a plain personal list and
    requires nobody else.

-   Add items to the list as with any shopping list app, and check them
    off yourself as you buy them.

-   Optionally invite up to three other people (phone number, email, or
    shareable link/code). Inviting someone into your solo group simply
    adds them to that existing group — the list is already there, so
    there is no merge step.

-   Accepting your first invite into someone else's group moves your
    solo group's open items into the joined group's list; the emptied
    solo group is then deleted.

-   Everyone in a group can add items, see everyone else's items, and
    mark any open item as purchased when they buy it — their own items
    included.

-   Marking an item purchased is per-item, not all-or-nothing — a
    shopper can fulfill some people's items on a trip and leave the rest
    open for someone else to pick up later.

-   A user who belongs to multiple groups sees one merged list, with
    each item's source group visually distinguished (color tag, group
    label, or grouped sections).

-   A user always has at least one group: if they ever leave or lose
    their last group, a fresh solo group is created automatically.

3. Groups & Membership

Group size: Maximum 4 active members per group. Implementation note:
this cap — and first-come-first-served waitlist promotion below — is
race-prone under concurrent joins and cannot be expressed as a plain
declarative database constraint; enforce it with a trigger or a
serialized insert check.

Membership model: Fully symmetric — no admin, no owner, no roles. Nobody
can remove anybody else. The only way to leave is to leave yourself.

Joining: By phone number, email, or shareable link/code. The invitee
must actively accept — invites are never automatic.

Invite expiry: 7 days, unless revoked earlier. An invite is tied to the
group, not the person who sent it, so it stays valid even if the inviter
later leaves the group.

Full groups: If a group already has 4 members and someone tries to join
via a still-valid link or code, they're placed on a waitlist and
notified automatically if a spot opens. Promotion is strictly
first-come-first-served by request timestamp.

Email-invited members: Since login is phone-based, an email-invited
person must eventually attach a phone number to fully participate.

Leaving a group

-   Any open (unpurchased) items the leaver added vanish for everyone
    remaining in the group.

-   Already-purchased items the leaver added survive for a 2-day grace
    period after departure, so the buyer isn't left holding an
    untracked, unpaid item. During the grace period, the departed
    member's name still shows on those items.

-   If the buyer of one of those items also leaves during the grace
    period, their name and the purchased record still stand — it's
    treated as a historical transaction record, not a live membership
    fact.

-   If the last member leaves a group, the group and its remaining data
    are deleted.

Blocking

Blocking uses the same vanish rules as a voluntary departure. Its
removal and notification effects are one-directional; its co-placement
bar is bidirectional (a deliberate change from v2):

-   When A blocks B, A leaves every group A and B share (A's open items
    vanish per the normal leave rule).

-   B is not removed from anything and is not notified that they've been
    blocked.

-   Neither A nor B can subsequently join a group that contains the
    other — via invite, link, code, or waitlist promotion. All such
    rejections are silent. A waitlist entry whose promotion would
    co-place a blocked pair (either direction) is skipped, and the next
    eligible entry is promoted instead.

-   Rationale for the two-way bar: a one-way bar would still let B join
    A's other groups, which defeats the point of blocking.

-   There is no ability to kick another member out of a group directly.

4. Items

An item moves through three states: Open → Purchased → Removed.

-   Only the person who added an item can edit its text or remove it
    from the list — this prevents an item from disappearing before the
    person who requested it has actually picked it up or paid for it.

-   Any active group member — including the person who added it — can
    mark an open item as purchased. Checking off your own items is the
    normal flow on a solo list, and common on shared ones (the shopper
    often buys their own items on the same trip).

-   If two people want the same item (e.g., you and your neighbor both
    want broccoli), it stays as two separate rows, one under each person
    — items are never merged.

-   Purchased items show who bought them and when, not just a checkmark,
    since that detail matters for settling up in person afterward.

-   Marking something purchased by mistake is recoverable: the buyer
    (and only the buyer) can unmark it back to Open.

-   Race condition: if two members buy the same open item close together
    before the app has synced, the app needs close-to-real-time sync and
    a graceful “already purchased” state for whoever's action arrives
    second, rather than a conflict or duplicate purchase.

-   No payment, IOU, or balance tracking happens in the app. A
    lightweight, non-financial “what I bought for others recently” view
    is included to reduce disputes without turning into a ledger.

5. Bulk Items — a headline feature

Group shopping is most valuable at bulk retailers like Costco or Sam's
Club, where quantities are too large for one person and splitting a case
among neighbors is the whole point. This is intended to be one of
Cartpool's main selling points, not a footnote.

-   Any item can be tagged as bulk/shared.

-   It's added once, by whoever is buying it.

-   Other group members opt in with a single tap — binary in/out, no
    quantity math. Payment for each person's share is settled outside
    the app, same as any other item.

-   Opt-in can happen either way: pre-commit before the item is bought
    (a request, e.g. “count me in if you get the 24-pack”), or after —
    the buyer can retroactively assign people to a bulk item they
    already purchased.

-   A free-text note field is available on bulk items (e.g. “get me the
    unsalted kind”), since there's deliberately no structured quantity
    or variant field.

-   If the adder edits a bulk item's text after someone has
    pre-committed, that person's opt-in must be reconfirmed — the edit
    could change what they're agreeing to.

6. Notifications

-   Instant, per-item push notifications — a buyer marking ten items
    purchased after a big Costco run means ten pings, and that's an
    accepted tradeoff for immediacy over batching.

-   A per-group mute toggle for anyone who finds a specific group's
    notifications overwhelming.

-   A single global mute toggle for someone who wants quiet by default
    and opts into notifications for specific groups instead.

7. Accessibility

A meaningful part of the value here is for elderly users living alone or
with limited mobility, who depend on a neighbor or family member to shop
for them. Accessibility is designed in from the start:

-   Large, generous tap targets.

-   A large-text display mode.

-   Minimal navigation depth — few taps between opening the app and
    adding or checking an item.

8. Identity, Privacy & Sign-In

-   Sign-in is phone-number based; the phone number is the single
    account identity across the app.

-   Other group members only ever see display names — phone numbers are
    never exposed to other members and are used solely for
    authentication and invite delivery.

-   Because identity is phone-based, reinstalling or switching devices
    is just a matter of re-authenticating by phone; the backend
    re-attaches the existing account and its group memberships.

9. Monetization

Free tier: Up to 3 groups. The solo group created at signup is a real
group and counts as one of the three from the moment the account is
created, even before any friends are added.

Paid tier: $5 every 3 months unlocks unlimited additional groups for
that period.

Downgrade handling: If a paid subscription lapses while the user belongs
to more than 3 groups, every one of their groups becomes read-only and a
required selection screen prompts them to choose exactly 3 to keep; the
screen re-appears on every app open until resolved. Once they confirm,
the kept 3 return to full read-write, and only the excess groups remain
read-only (not deleted) — restored in full if the user resubscribes.

Subscription billing implementation

Handled via RevenueCat (or bare StoreKit/Play Billing) managing a
$5/3-month auto-renewing subscription product on both the App Store and
Play Store. RevenueCat handles receipt validation, renewal,
cancellation, and cross-device restore. Because login is
phone-number-based rather than email/password, the backend needs to link
each store receipt to the phone-number account so entitlement follows
the user across devices and reinstalls.

-   Billing grace period: both stores offer a retry window before a
    lapsed card cancels entitlement (roughly 16–21 days on Apple,
    similar on Google). This should be turned on — the user base skews
    elderly, and a stale expired card shouldn't silently trigger the
    “choose 3 groups” downgrade flow.

-   Refunds: the store processes the refund itself; RevenueCat's refund
    webhook should flip the account's paid status to false and
    re-trigger the downgrade-to-3 flow, same as a normal cancellation.

-   Device/reinstall continuity: since identity is phone-based, a
    reinstall or new device re-authenticates by phone first, the backend
    re-establishes the same internal user ID, and RevenueCat's
    restore-purchases call re-attaches the subscription to that ID
    automatically — no manual “restore purchase” button should be
    necessary.

-   Apple Family Sharing: allowed. Entitlement is per phone number
    rather than per household, so enabling it costs nothing and is a
    reasonable nice-to-have.

-   Implementation specifics: Two RevenueCat products are defined, one
    per store, both mapped to a single RevenueCat entitlement (e.g.
    cartpool_unlimited). At login, RevenueCat's app_user_id is set to
    the backend's internal user ID (keyed off phone number), so client
    state and server state share one identity with no separate mapping
    table. The backend subscribes to RevenueCat's webhook
    (INITIAL_PURCHASE, RENEWAL, CANCELLATION, EXPIRATION, BILLING_ISSUE,
    REFUND), verifies the webhook signature, and writes entitlement
    status onto that user record. As a fallback for a missed webhook,
    the client also calls RevenueCat's REST API to re-sync entitlement
    on app foreground.

10. Explicitly Out of Scope (v1)

-   In-app payments, IOUs, or running balances of any kind — only the
    non-financial “what I bought for others” history.

-   In-app messaging or chat — item-specific questions (“what kind of
    bell pepper?”) happen on whatever texting app the two people already
    use.

-   Quantity math on bulk items — opt-in is binary plus a free-text
    note, nothing more structured.

-   Sorting or tagging items by store section or aisle (produce, dairy,
    household, etc.).

-   Any ability to remove or kick another member from a group.

-   An in-app resolution path for a lost or never-handed-off purchased
    item (buyer marks it purchased, recipient never actually gets it).
    This is treated as a real-world dispute to be handled over text, not
    an app bug to fix later.

11. Data Retention

-   Removed items and expired invites are purged after 2 weeks.

-   Grace-period purchased items from a departed member are removed 2
    days after departure unless a shorter rule above applies.

12. Phase 2 / Future Ideas

Surplus broadcasts

A feature to post “I've got extra broccoli, anyone interested?” to a
group. This is deliberately scoped as its own lightweight post type
rather than folded into the item model, since it behaves differently
from a normal list item: there's no single adder, it's group-wide
visible, and it needs a claim/close mechanic plus an expiration or
manual close. Worth designing properly in a later phase.

-   Claim flow: Interested group members tap to express interest; the
    poster reviews and manually chooses who gets the surplus item,
    rather than first-come-first-served. This lets the poster split a
    surplus among multiple people or account for who actually needs it.

-   Closing: Only the poster can close a broadcast — consistent with the
    item model, where only the adder can edit or remove. They close it
    once it's been handed off, or to cancel it.

-   Auto-expiry: If the poster hasn't closed it, the post auto-expires
    48 hours after posting and drops out of the group's active view.

Usage analytics

Anonymous tracking of which features are most and least used, to guide
future prioritization. Not part of the initial build.

-   Privacy posture: Aggregated event counts only — no per-user or
    per-device identifier of any kind. The data can answer “how many
    times was feature X used this week” but never “what did user Y do,”
    which rules out funnel or retention analysis by design in exchange
    for the data being structurally non-reidentifiable.

Amendment v3.1 — July 21, 2026: Monetization change

Supersedes the "$5 every 3 months" paid tier in §9 and the auto-renewing
subscription mechanics under "Subscription billing implementation".

New model:

-   Every account gets unlimited groups for 3 months from account
    creation (trial_ends_at, set at signup).
-   After that, holding more than the 3 free groups requires a single
    one-time $10 purchase ("unlock unlimited lists") — a lifetime
    entitlement, not a subscription. No renewals, expirations,
    cancellations, or billing grace periods exist.
-   Trial expiry while over the limit funnels into the unchanged v3
    downgrade flow: read-only everywhere → pick exactly 3 to keep →
    excess groups stay read-only. The purchase clears any freeze or
    pick without re-picking; nothing is ever deleted.
-   Refunds (store-processed, via RevenueCat webhook) revoke the
    entitlement; if the user is past their trial and over the limit,
    the same downgrade flow triggers. A refund during the trial changes
    nothing until the trial ends.
-   Store products: one non-consumable (Apple) / one-time in-app
    product (Google) at $10, both mapped to the single RevenueCat
    entitlement cartpool_unlimited. Webhook events reduce to
    INITIAL_PURCHASE / NON_RENEWING_PURCHASE / REFUND. Family Sharing,
    device-continuity, and app_user_id mapping carry over unchanged.

Amendment v3.2 — July 21, 2026: "Up for grabs" (surplus sharing)

New feature. Over-sized packs (typically online bulk purchases) leave the
buyer with extras; this lets them shed the surplus inside their group.

-   A member posts surplus units: item text + how many are available.
    "I bought 4 nail clippers — anybody want the other 3?"
-   Any groupmate can claim units while some remain. Claims are per-unit
    and one person may claim more than one: with 3 available, Bill may
    take 1, 2, or all 3 (repeat claims accumulate). Claiming decrements
    availability atomically — the same serialization discipline as the
    purchase race — so the last unit goes to exactly one person.
-   Claimers can give back part or all of a claim while the offer is
    open. The poster can take the offer down early; claims already made
    stand. Offers expire after 14 days; expired/closed offers purge 14
    days later. A member leaving a group closes their open offers and
    releases their claims on others'.
-   Pricing is the poster's choice and is a LABEL only: free, at cost,
    or a named per-unit price. The app suggests the at-cost number
    (pack price ÷ pack size) but never records who paid — consistent
    with the no-IOU/no-ledger rule (§10). Handoff and any money are
    settled offline.
-   Non-goals: no payment tracking, no "mark as paid", no cross-group
    or public marketplace. Offers are visible only inside the group.

Amendment v3.3 — July 22, 2026: Cross-group items

Supersedes the per-group item model implied throughout §2 and §4, the
group targeting of new items, and the "open items vanish" leave rule in
§3. Implemented in migration 0013.

The change in one sentence: an item belongs to its ADDER, not to a
group — it is one canonical record, automatically visible on the list
of every group the adder is in, and buying it anywhere clears it
everywhere.

Visibility (the adder's pool)

-   An item is visible to everyone who shares at least one active group
    with the person who added it (the adder's "pool"), including the
    adder themselves. There is no per-group targeting and no group
    picker: you add an item once and it appears, automatically, on the
    shared list of every group you belong to.
-   The pool is evaluated live, not at add time: join a new group and
    your existing open items appear there too; the reverse on leaving
    (see below).
-   Two members of the adder's pool who don't share a group with EACH
    OTHER both see the adder's items, but still never see one
    another's. Visibility always chains through the adder, never
    transitively.
-   Items retain a "home group" (where they were added) as provenance.
    It no longer gates visibility of open items; its only user-facing
    role is the departure grace window below.

Purchase anywhere

-   Any member of the adder's pool — from any of the adder's groups —
    can mark an open item purchased. Because the item is a single
    record, the first purchase clears it from every list simultaneously;
    there is nothing to cascade and no cross-group double-buy state.
-   The purchase race rule from §4 is unchanged and now spans groups:
    if two people in different groups buy the same item close together,
    exactly one wins and the other gets the graceful "already purchased
    by {name}" state.
-   Buyer and time are recorded as before; the buyer (and only the
    buyer) can unmark. Adder-only edit/remove is unchanged.
-   Write eligibility follows the same subscription rules as before
    (§9, v3.1): the buyer must share at least one group with the adder
    in which the buyer can write. Frozen accounts remain read-only
    everywhere.
-   Bulk items follow the pool too: opt-in (pre-commit or after
    purchase) and retroactive assignment by the buyer are open to
    anyone who shares a group with the adder, not just the home group's
    members.

The "To pick up" view

-   When someone else buys one of your items, it moves to a "To pick
    up" section pinned at the top of your list, labeled with who bought
    it ("Pick up from {name}") and when — you should never have to hunt
    for who is holding your groceries.
-   An item leaves the section when the buyer unmarks it (mistake
    recovery, back to Open) or when you remove it (normal adder-only
    removal, e.g. once collected and settled).
-   The main screen is now one unified list rather than per-group
    sections: To pick up, then open items, then purchase history.
    Rows keep showing who each item is for.

Leaving a group (revised semantics)

-   Your open items are yours: leaving a group no longer deletes them.
    They stay on your own list (and on the lists of any groups you
    remain in); ex-groupmates simply stop seeing them, because
    visibility follows shared membership.
-   Purchased items keep the 2-day grace window (§3), reframed: an item
    of yours already bought by a groupmate stays visible to the old
    group's members for 2 days — the buyer isn't left holding an
    untracked, unpaid item — after which it leaves their view but
    remains in YOUR purchase history instead of being deleted.
-   Blocking is unchanged (§3): it still works by the blocker leaving
    shared groups, and inherits these leave semantics.

Non-goals

-   No per-item audience control ("only show this to group X") — that
    reintroduces the targeting decision this amendment removes. If a
    request is private to one circle, that's what separate accounts or
    a future phase-2 idea is for.
-   No merging of duplicate wants across adders (§4 rule stands: same
    item wanted by two people = two rows).
